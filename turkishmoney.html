Traveling yields new experiences, new places and new types of money. Every country has their own currency and Turkey is no exception. Their Lira is a quite interesting form of money, as we will now look at.

The Turkish one hundred
thousand Lira bill.
What makes the Lira so different from other money? The Turkish Lira differs from most other currencies in that its denominations are so large. Even their smallest bill numbers at five thousand. However, these high denominations aren't backed by equally large values. As a matter of fact, that hundred thousand isn't even worth a quarter. There are approximately five hundred thousand Turkish Lira to one United States Dollar.


Two hundred fifty thousand
Lira - worth fifty cents.
Since this currency deals with such large numbers, people who are accustomed to US money might find it intimidating to purchase something costing five million Lira when, in actuality, it is a mere ten dollars they would end up parting with. You definitely shouldn't show fear of purchasing so, for your convenience, the following example illustrates converting Dollars to Lira.


Though worth near to nothing,
the bill's detail is extensive.

Take your dollar amount, for example 3 dollars and 35 cents. Multiply the number by 500,000.

3.35 US dollars * 500,000 Lira = 1,675,000 Lira

It's that simple. While the exchange rate fluctuates a few thousand Lira every day, it is but mere pennies and the method outlined above will allow a quick rough price of an object. However for those that aren't mathematically inclined, it is advisable to carry a calculator or currency converter with you to quickly evaluate prices.


This five million Lira bill
is worth ten US Dollars.
Many people have wondered why the Lira has such large denominations. The answer is inflation. When the currency becomes less valuable, more of that currency is needed to buy things. This happened in post WWI Germany where inflation was so high that people took suitcases to work to collect their salary. While the problem is not that bad here, there are methods of solving the usage of large denominations.

The only way to rid the money of its zeros is a world-wide currency recall. The old money would stop being used and smaller denominations of the same value would be issued. This was done in Mexico several years ago and has worked well. The only problem is that a project of this sort is a long and involved one, something the Turkish government might not be willing to devote time, and ultimately money, to. For now we'll just have to live with the old denomination, chart popping Lira.
