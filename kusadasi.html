Although it is not evident today, in ancient times the Basilica of St. John was part of the city of Ephesus. The Basilica was once topped with 11 domes and rivaled Istanbul's Hagia Sophia in scale. It was built by Emperor Justinian and is one of the magnificent monuments of the Middle Ages. Standing down the hill from St. John's is a single column, the only remaining portion of the Temple of Artemis, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. This pagan temple was torn down once Christianity was adopted. Interestingly, most of the marble pillars from the temple were carried to Istanbul to be used in the construction of the Hagia Sophia.

Both of Jesus' disciples John and Paul lived in Ephesus as did the Virgin Mary. Paul spent six years preaching about Christianity from his workshop in Ephesus. John spent the last years of his life in Ephesus and wrote the fourth book of the New Testament here. And Mary found refuge after the death of Jesus in a peaceful forested valley nearby. From the very first days of Christianity, this site has been a holy place of pilgrimage.

Once the glorious Roman capital of Asia Minor, it was in Ephesus that Mark Antony and Cleopatra rode in procession, St. Paul preached against the goddess Artemis, and St. John and the Virgin Mary spent their last days. The history of Ephesus is so incredibly extensive!

At its height Ephesus was an extremely well-off Roman seaside town and a major commercial center with 300,000 inhabitants. As can be assumed, Ephesus thrived because of its port-status, living off its imports and exports. However, as the years past, the sea receded, and with that went the city's popularity.

Following the map-picture to the right, we entered the city a little before the Basilica and proceeded to look around the Odeon, which is a small amphitheather capable of seating fifteen hundred people. The constructor, Vedius Antonius, built it to hold small concerts and plays. After touring the Odeon we slowly traveled forward, admiring the remains of ancient Roman architecture all around us.

Sitting in this area was the Temple of Domitian, constructed to honor Emperor Domitian. This two-floored building contained warehouses and shops on the first floor and the temple on the second floor.

The library was built in 135 A.D. under the order of C. Julius in honor of his father, C. Celsus who had been the governor of Asia Minor. Leading up to the library are four statues, two of which symbolize Justice and Virtue. If one heads to the back of the library you can see the tomb of Celsus, a stunning white marble sarcophagus. We hung around the library for a good fifteen to twenty minutes taking pictures and admiring the beauty of this ancient structure. The cruise staff took a group photo of us as well, but that is unavailable for viewing. When time ran short we headed down the long Marble Road to our next stop, the Great Amphitheater.


 The Great Amphitheater from afar.
Our guide unlocked the metal gate separating us and the massive stone theater. We were allowed to enter and look around for a bit before the guide taught us about it. The theater could house 25,000 people. Imagine yourself being in the audience when Paul made his famous speech to the Ephesians.

The theater is still used today. Musicians have held concerts in it. We were informed that another concert would take place in the theater for the Millenium Celebration. Slowly we exited the theater and headed down the Stadium Road to meet our buses.

Our tour of Ephesus and surrounds had one more stop, the House of the Virgin Mary. After Jesus died, Mary came to Ephesus with John who looked after her. She lived here on a secluded hill in the woods until she died around 35 A.D. We walked through the tiny house where there is a peaceful shrine with a statue of her, which was found when the ruins of the house were discovered. We then headed down the steps to the fountains, which are said to have life restoring elements. Everyone drank from the holy fountains and some stopped to tie a piece of paper to the Prayer Wall. Leaving the holy grounds we made our way to the buses which were now cruise ship bound.

We returned to the ship at about four o'clock leaving the whole afternoon to ourselves. Everyone took this time to relax and reflect on the day. It was casual attire for dinner, so everyone got dressed and we met at seven for cocktails in the Starlight Lounge. Following dinner everyone parted for the evening to retire to their cabins, still enthralled by the day's sights.
